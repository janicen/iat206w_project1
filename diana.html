Brazil

Looking at the elements of national identity and multiculturalism in Brazil, this study focuses on the popular cultures deeply embedded in Brazilian culture. Then, the possibility of methods to translate American comic book hero, Captain America, are proposed. The goal of doing so is to adapt the character in a new cultural context, hence the new character is more appropriate for Brazilian culture. The popular cultures of Brazil in discussion are football, graphic novel, film and radio overall. Each of these popular cultures is analyzed using conceptual frameworks consisting of media concepts: color, meme, comic, color, television, embodiment, imagination, globalization, hybridity and remediation. The questions in this paper are: What has contributed to modern Brazil and current pop culture? How this culture emerged and what kind of characteristics of popular culture are displayed associated with national identity of Brazil? Throughout the research, the phenomena found in popular cultures are questioned and examined to discover comparison and contrast to Captain America. This method lead to suggestions for potential design in reshaping Captain America embodying/reflecting to the Brazilian culture.

Comic Book Pretense


The reason for choosing to research Brazil and Captain America was due to its’ potential market. According to Box Office Mojo, the total net grossing of Captain America: The First Avenger (2011) in Brazil was $20,681,912. This amount is one of the top four among foreign countries. Based on this data, our group decided to look at top four countries and study one of four countries. The objective is to translate character of Captain America into the context of four countries: United Kingdom, Russia, Brazil and Mexico.

The focus here is to analyze popular cultures in Brazil and identifying popular cultures, which displays national identity and motif. The graphic novels, football, architecture, television and radio are discussed in terms of cultural and media concepts. Who is Captain America?


Before going into research, it is important to learn who Captain America is and what are the elements that consists of his identity. Created by two American cartoonist Joe Simon and Jack Kirby back in 1940s, Captain America is a fictional character and a superhero from a comic book which later was adapted to various media forms such as films, games, television, music, theatre and referenced throughout many others. For this research, the focus will be on Captain America: The First Avenger(2011) film.

Captain America, also known as Steve Rogers is a national symbol and patriotic soldier and a hero in the historical background of World War II. He meets a challenge in enlisting for US army due to his lack of physical ability to pass the examination to become a soldier, however he displays courage and willingness to protect and sacrifice himself for others. His bravery and strong will allows him to be selected as candidate for a project by US army to create super soldier, using “Super Soldier Serum.” Captain America has qualities and an ideal representation of national identity of US through design of his physique, attire, colors, shield, and his personality. Now, the question is how can to learn from this character and translate it to another culture. In this case, Brazilian culture.

Globalization and Cultural Hybridity


Multiculturalism is deeply rooted in the culture of Brazil including history of Portuguese colonization of Latin America, slavery of African and Indigenous people, and immigration of Asian population. Located in Eastern South America, current Brazil society consists of White, Mulatto, Black, Asian and Indigenous people. Alex Bellos (2014) explains, “Brazil is the country of carnival, not of self-negating uniformity” (p. 34). With Black population making up around 7.6% of the population and mix of European traditions and African traditions, Brazilian population together built many of today’s traditions and customs such as football, samba music, dance and carnival.

Imagination - Folk tale hero - Malandro


Throughout the media in Brazil, topic of Malandro, a folk hero and a person of Brazilian malandragem stereotype can be found in the sports, samba music and lyrics. It is a significant symbolism rooted in Brazilian media.  João Gabriel L. C. Teixeira (2003) explains malandragem’s moral principle focuses on forming idealistic world where ‘tolerance and and unpredictability’ is possible in life (p. 32). Malandragem represent a lifestyle of a rogue or a con artist who result to trickery in their way of life. This type of image influences on various media as a heroic figure. It  became an important part of Brazilian national identity such as Brazilian football.

Many scholarly articles and books examining football culture in Brazil often mentions malandro in discussion of the sports culture and national identity. Bellos (2014) mentions that malandro transformed style of football from ‘an orderly British game’ to ‘a dance of irrational surprises’ (p.36). The influence of malandro seems to define Brazilian identities besides the Brazilian football style as well. In An Ethnography of the 2006 World Cup in Brazilian Cities, Édison Gastaldo explains, “[in] Brazil, soccer is always called ‘jogo’, a term that defines both gambling and sports, while in England the two words define completely different activities” (Gastaldo, 2007, p. 14). As it defines Brazilian football distinctively different from the original European definition of the sport, maladragem helped developed a distinctive national identity known as ‘Brazilian-ness’. The term Malandro can be found in samba music as well. It will be discussed in Samba Music and Dance section later.

Football - radio, television


The media which allowed spread of sports event was through radio and television.  Édison Gastaldo (2014) informs that:

Since the beginning of the regular international transmission of soccer matches on the radio, in the early 1950s, the coverage of Brazil’s matches in the World Cup has produced amazing audiences....a radio station in São Paulo installed a huge panel painted as a soccer field covered with lamps in the Praça da Sé (a large square in the centre of the city), with a public sound system transmitting live on their own radio. (p. 126)

The games are reached mass audience using radio first, then by television. The radio primarily relies on the voice of broadcaster narrating the play. In contrast to that, the television captures the game by portraying in addition to broadcaster’s voice narrating in the background. Gastaldo (2014) explains, “...with dozens of still and mobile cameras spread out all over the field, showing many different angles of the match, this media narrative is even more emphatic....effectively broadcasted are chosen according to a peculiar codification of television narrative...” (p. 124). Gastaldo brings up example of several replay of goal scene, which emphasizes the moment and the excitement of the scene. Highlighting emotional aspect of the game, the medium of radio and television delivers different perspective of the game and audience engagement. One with relaying on sound. Latter one relaying on both image and sound. The move from radio to television brought opportunity to reach national and global audience as well. Globalization and popularity of football throughout the world had made it possible for soccer players to become pop icon in the global stage, therefore creating business opportunity to market national identities. Marcos Alvito (2007) states, vigorous push to transform football into ‘mega-business’ happened as ‘Brazilian’ João Havelange was appointed as FIFA president in 1974 (p. 527). However, there has been negative effects of globalization of football. The diaspora happened with soccer players moving to play for teams outside of Brazil. Alvito (2007) explains, “In 2004 alone, according to official data published by the Brazilian Football Confederation, 857 football players were transferred to teams in other countries” (p. 531). Referring to this data, Alvito analyzes how the influence of ‘football diaspora’ — fall of ‘technical level’ — in Brazil due to outsourcing players happened, and broadcasting of sport on television lead to low number of audiences for the sport. Also, the globalization of sport left the country to face various social problems.

Samba Music and Dance 


Along with malandragem and football, samba is an essence of Brazil’s national identity. In Encyclopædia Britannica, it is explained that “Samba crossed the colour line and rose to national popularity through the radio and recording industries in the 1940s” (“Latin American dance,” 2015). The Google search of samba images shows many dark-skinned women dancers and male drummers. Their facial expression and body gesture embodies energy and vibrance. In Rio Carnival, dancers celebrates samba in an organized extravagant parade with music and exquisite costumes with vibrant colors, feathers and many jewelry. The carnival serves as  a spectating purposes, but it also is a big competition between many samba schools. The history of samba goes back to days in slavery. According to “Latin American dance” in Encyclopædia Britannica:

After slavery ended in 1888, sugarcane workers migrated to the cities; many of them settled on the hills surrounding Rio de Janeiro. These favelas (shantytowns) became the incubators for Rio Carnival samba, as its inhabitants organized themselves into escolas de samba (“samba schools,” which functioned as community-based clubs). (2015)

Today, the culture only shared between slaves became national music. The cultural diaspora through slavery and mix of European culture had significant influence in their media. The history of slavery and the ancestors of Afro-Brazilian people become a root of samba music, customs, and dances.

Comic 


Another popular culture in the country is comic industry. There are talented Brazilian artists, who work at well-known comic companies in United States such as DC comics.  Lisa Shaw and Stephanie Dennison (2005) states that “[along] with the United States and Japan, Brazil is one of the world’s three largest market for comics, known in Brazil as história em quadrinhos or gibis ” (p.170).  Start of comic in Brazil began with newspapers containing illustrations and caricatures in eighteenth century. These illustrations and caricatures often contained criticism about society and political rule. Such influence in comic is due to censorship and limited freedom of expression starting with coup d'état of 1964 and emerge of military dictatorship around 1970s. Many cartoons criticizing politics or social fad were published in magazines from publishing companies such as Ciro Editorial. Roberto Elísio do Santos (2007) tells that artists at Circo often focused on ‘urban middle class‘ as they attempted to portray of foolishness of ‘society dominating classes‘ using ‘comic humor’ (p. 112). The cartoons from the time after dictatorship still continued to contain ‘comical humor’ and focused on topics immediately relating to the reality of culture. Similar phenomenon occurs in the television in Brazil. The ‘narrative mode’ of telenovela — type of serialized drama—   after 1980s “emphasized immediacy and actuality rather than fantasy and remoteness.” (Hamburger, 2003, p. 107). Similar to comic by Circo Editorials, the telenovela focuses on reality and reflects upon the current issues. Esther hamburger (2003) explains:

Staged in contemporary times, shot in the studios, but including external sequences shot on location, incorporating contemporary slang and fashion, making references to social and political events and to real places, novelas retained the original melodramatic narrative structure, but situated the stories in contemporary times and in well defined spaces. (p. 107)

With cartoon style comics, Brazilian artists and illustrator contributed in making of original comic or illustrations for big companies such as DC comics. Many artist worked on well-known American comics such as Green Lantern, Teen Titans, Batman, The Shield and many more.

Brazilian Comics Examples 


Most known piece of comic strip is Monica’s Gang by Mauricio de Sousa who had a huge success with the comic and ended up franchising the story of a girl named Monica and her friends. It has been remediated into various video games and films. “Mauricio de Sousa's beloved comic books are a staple of many Brazilian childhoods” (Manthei, 2011, p. 142). He has many other comic characters loved by many Brazilian children.

Another interesting comic is Capitão Brasil by Julian Rock. It is a short comic strip about adventure of character who portrays characteristic of malandro through his nonsensical behaviour. In daily life, he seems to be a generic office worker. Even when he is in super hero attire, Captain Brazil does not display characteristic of heroic figure.

Rock, J. (2013). Capitão Brasil [Images of comic strip]. Retrieved on February, 10, 2015, from: http://capitaobrasil.com.br/

He is mainly portrayed as a sloth (homem-preguiça) who wears a suit designed in  colors of yellow and green, which immediately recalls Brazil’s national flag. With blue sphere symbol from the flag, a letter B is embedded to represent Brazil. This part is similar to Captain America’s suit design following colors of American flag. Although he has superhero like appearance, his deeds seem to appear comical and troublesome than reliable or helpful.

Rock, J. (2012). Capitão Brasil [Images of comic strip]. Retrieved on February, 10, 2015, from: http://capitaobrasil.com.br/

Only time when heroic characteristic seems to be embodied by this character is when  he displays super power such as deflection of bullets with his body, flying and few other abilities. He attempts to help out people with their ordeal, however it often results to a further complication of the event or a comical result. As for other characters in the comic, there are mainly people in need of assistance and an antagonist figure, a pizza man. Often, the comic strip includes hero and heroine from another comic such as Spider man.

Besides Capitão Brasil with style similar to American comic, there are comics with styles incorporating Japanese art style. Although Japanese are small portion of the whole population, the aesthetic of Japanese comic style are replicated in comics by Brazilian artists. Web comics such as Combo Rangers (1998) by Fábio Yabu and XDragoon (2008) by Felipe Marcantonio contains aesthetic following Japanese art style often used in Japanese comic known as manga. Similar to Japanese Super Sentai series - meant to be designed for long-run - such as Power Rangers, Combo Rangers have distinctive colors representing each protagonists.

Brazilian comic includes dynamic styles since it integrates many different art styles and cultural elements. One example is when Japanese culture is presented using Japanese names for characters in Brazilian comics. In Mauricio de Sousa's Chico Bento,  Japanese-Brazilian boy appears. “Hiro, a Japanese Brazilian boy, wears shoes and a cap (a marker of urbanity); he has more material goods and urban savvy than Chico, and is described as preserving Japanese cultural elements (MSP, "Hiro")” (Manthei, 2011, p. 146). Here, the part of minority culture has been included in comic of well-known Brazilian comic artist. This further shows the importance of minority culture in the popular culture of Brazil.

Conclusion


From European colonist to World Cup, the modern Brazil has rich and complex community born from cultural phenomena associating issues of globalization, social inequalities and popular sports. These popular culture contains ideas, which are closely associated with the national identity. The complexity born from the multi-cultural society brings a lot of tension in the society. Brazil shows strong contrast between the poor and rich landscapes. Brazilian national identity has many layers. It more than just elite white culture. There are Afro-Brazilian, Asian and Indigenous communities. Indeed, Brazil is an country with unique concepts in each media.
