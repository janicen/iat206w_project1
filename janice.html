Mexico

By using the primary culture found through Captain America, this section utilizes cultural and media concepts to approach a cultural study on the country of Mexico from understanding the behaviour and people of Mexico to its historical and modern events. Throughout the essay, it begins by defining the concepts that makes up the country, followed by its technological advancements, then narrowing to the overarching principle of superheroes of Mexico. Hence, this relates to how Captain America has been defined as an individual, the technologies used to create him as a superhuman, as well as the character in becoming a superhero. As a result, within the cultural domain of superheroes, the concepts of identity, nationalism, symbol, interface, network, graphic novel, television, film, translation, genre are used as a framework in exploring the Mexican country to derive a new superhero, such as a creation of Captain Mexico.

Overall, Mexico is seen with either a Hispanic or Latino identity through its historical development, but most identifies themselves as Mexicans where identity “…is caused by a conflation of discrete phenomena.” through groups, cultural identities or ethnic categories that are always changing (p. 110, 2007, Eriksen). As described by Schmal (2004), “[t]he Mexican Republic of the Twenty-First Century, boasting more than 100 million inhabitants, has evolved from many indigenous national five centuries ago into a single national entity, with Spanish as its primary language.” (para. 1, 2004, Schmal). As a result, a 2011 survey was conducted by the Pew Hispanic Center and discovered that “…52% of U.S. Hispanics of Mexican origin usually described themselves as “Mexican” or “Mexico,” while 26% described themselves as Hispanic or Latino and 19% most often said they were American.” (para. 2, 2013, Desilver) as seen in the graph below.

Pew Research Center. (2011). Mexican-American Views about Identity, by Generation [Survey Infographic]. Retrieved on February, 17, 2015, from: http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2013/05/03/how-mexicans-in-the-united-states-see-their-identity/

With the graph portrayed, this is a mean of social identification as Turner (1972) suggested that to define an individual, “…the individual’s knowledge that he belongs to certain social groups together with some emotional and value significance to him of the group membership…” (p. 18, 1982, Tajfel). On a side note, an official adoption of the terms “Hispanic” and “Latino” occurred after numerous years of “…lobbying by Mexican-American and Hispanic organizations…” the law was passed in 1976 under the name of Joint resolution relating to the public of economic and social statistics for Americans of Spanish origin or descendent (para. 15, 2012, Lopez et al.). However, when a Mexican describe their identity, most relate to “…their family’s country of origin over pan-ethnic terms.” where statistic shows that 51% of the population would use terms such as “Mexican” while 24% uses terms such as “Hispanic” or “Latino” and 21% would simply identify themselves as “American” (para. 7, 2012, Lopez et al.). On the other hand, an example of the loyalty of one’s Mexican identity derives from the Cinco de Mayo (fifth of May) day which is “…widely celebrated in the United States, and not just among the 33.7 million U.S. residents of Mexican origin [but it is to] commemorates the 1862 victory of Mexican forces over an invading French army at the battle of Puebla.” (para. 1, 2013, Desilver). Alternatively, even though the term Hispanic and Latino were established, Mexicans only identifies themselves as the following only if they self identifies themselves (para. 17, 2012, Lopez et al.) leading to a mix cultural identity to create nationalism.

Nationalism in Mexico began upon “…a discourse of self-affirmation articulated by the criollo (Creole) elite in the late eighteenth century.” (p. 279, Peña), which elaborated itself after gaining “…independence from Spain and formed a Republic.” (para. 1, 2012, Phillpott) in order to strengthen its country. However, the country has been trying to rebuild itself ever since the invasion using the methods of educational endeavors with missionaries teaching language and religion (para. 35, Petrovic). Hence, the country requires a link to bring individuals together such that “[h]istorically, an important part played by nationalist ideologies in contemporary nation-states has been to integrate an ever larger number of people culturally, politically and economically.” (p. 25, 2007, Eriksen) through education. Next, during the colonial period, the aim of the Spanish Crown was to create a civilization of the indigenous population such that Heath noted in 1972, “[c]omplete linguistic and cultural assimilation was essential to the Crown’s goal of one culture, one language, and one religion.” (para. 38, Petrovic). During the nineteenth century, while Mexico is starting to develop its nationalism, the creation of “…the new national unity stirred debate…liberals and conservatives struggled against each other for influence.” where each group had their own beliefs in driving a nation-state and means of promoting it (para. 42, Petrovic). Eventually, for Mexico to transform itself, the country began to focus on various types of nation-state, as described by Diaz-Couder in 1997, this includes “…an open economy, integration into the international market place, stability to attract foreign investment, and increased democracy.” (para. 1, Petrovic). On the other hand, the most successful method was to promote nationalism through education where Mexicans embrace multiculturalism, especially in assigning Spanish as a required language to preserve its cultural roots in the country (para. 2, Petrovic). Also, by doing so, it “…provides evidence of the importance of respecting [every person’s] backgrounds…” especially their linguistic backgrounds (para. 32, Petrovic). In order to obtain this state, Solana explained in 1981 that the country had to take control on education, as well as creating “…the Constitution of 1857…stating that education would be free and that the offices needed to implement it would be determined by law.” (para. 45, Petrovic). By celebrating the individuals of mestizaje, meaning mix in Spanish, the country can be developed in a constitutional manner. Furthermore, by integrating the society from a larger scale, it is achieved “…through the imposition of a uniform system of education, the introduction of universal contractual wagework, standardization of language and so forth…” which drives the goal of nationalism (p. 25, 2007, Eriksen). This evokes how one is part of a nationalism where the social distinction is made by having “…insiders and outsiders; between citizens and non-citizens.” (p. 26, 2007, Eriksen). As nationalism is not based on personal loyalty, it grows with its inhabitants, developing the country and the individual’s identities (p. 26, 2007, Eriksen) where Mexico has gone through this process to build its strength.

Within the historic events in developing the nationality of Mexico, various symbolic items are used to represent Mexico which forms its “…most fundamental division of signs (para, 9, Heuning). In terms of its name, it has been derived “…from the Aztecs, also known as Mexicas, who were warrior tribes that came from a place called Aztlán, thought to be in the northwestern region of Mexico.” (p. 1, SRE). Furthermore, the meaning of its name came from the Aztec olden Nahuati spoken language, known as “Place in the center of the moon” which has now became the famous Mexico City (p. 1, SRE), acting as the heart of Mexico. As for the flag, the design “…is taken from the French Tricolor flag.”, in addition, its colours indicate the national liberation army of Mexico (para. 1, World Atlas) upon the War of Independence with Spain during the years of 1810 to 1821 (p. 2, SRE). In order to obtain its tricolour, it was also through the “…several different Mexican groups fighting for the creation of a new nation.” which formed the army of the Three Guarantees upon agreement to having “…an independent monarchy, one religion, and the union of all Mexican people.” (p. 2, SRE). Hence, the symbolic colours of the flag reflect on the historic events such that green represents independence such as hope and prosperity, white represents the roman catholic religion, such as peace and harmony, while red represents the union of Spaniards and Mexicans, which is the blood of Mexican heroes (Mexico Flag) as the flag portrays below.

Furthermore, the coat of arm features “...a Mexican Golden Eagle perched on a prickly pear cactus eating a snake.” which also has a powerful religious connotation to its people to demonstrate the goods (para. 2, World Atlas) as seen above, which is the same icon found at the center of the flag. In terms of its historical story, a legend was told that the origin people of Aztecs wanted to leave home to start a new life where their God told them “…to find a lake where an eagle with a snake in its beak would stand on a nopal cactus growing from a rock.” to build their new city and that after much travelling, the arrival of the Valley of Mexico has now become Mexico City (p. 2, SRE) as sketched out in the historic times.

Moving on, in the electronic industry of Mexico, it has been seen with a high focus in the creation of physical devices to run digital interfaces where this concept is “…to understand connections between the human and the machine…” by bringing the study of the virtual and physical space together (p. 53, 2008, Beer & Gane). Ever since 1970, there were many Mexican companies that were producing “…computer and telephony equipment, processing units or laptop computers…” (para. 4, 2011, Pais). However, the boom of technology caused a big gap between Latin American countries in comparison to first world countries, but with influences and a higher support in education, the technological growth has expanded once again which Latin America such as Mexico can soon catch up (para. 2, Heermann et al.) and become the leader in technological production within the Latin American countries in the Twentieth Century. Also, Mexico has the responsibility in exporting at least $71.4 billion US dollars of consumer electronics and devices since 2010 (para. 2, 2011, Pais). Then, this peak has transformed the electronic industry in Mexican companies to now produce a high demand in home appliances, cell phones, computers, flat screen television and video games (para. 4, 2011, Pais). For instance, the company Meebox located in Mexico creates personal computers, tablets and mobile devices using the latest interfaces from Microsoft and Android. Furthermore, as Mexico is a leading country in electronic production, Meebox is the first Mexican company to manufacture a completely functional tablet computer and Windows tablet (Rubio, 2011). In the ancient times of Mexico, illustrations and comic strips were already present under “…a long tradition of pictorial storytelling in Mexico that dates back to pre-Hispanic times.” (para. 1, 2013, Critchley). Despite the popularity of comic books in Mexico, cartoonists believed that it is considered as low class work (para. 13, 2013, Critchley) where there were originally no intension to improve the basic storytelling method of a comic to a graphic novel. However, one day, “…Josefina Larragoiti’s publishing firm Editorial Resistencia has become a pioneer of publishing graphic novels, a genre previously ignored by the mainstream…” (para. 1, 2013, Critchley) where their mission is “…to open a niche for young, unknown writers…” (para. 3, 2013, Critchley). Here, it is seen that the powerful medium of “[g]raphic novels combines text and pictures equally in order to convey a narrative.” can portray meaningful stories where the visual representation of a story are introduced by sequential placement of words from dialogue or descriptive writing, as well as images of the art and design (para. 15, Thompson). Surprisingly, their company received a bombardment of manuscripts of essays, children’s stories and graphic novels (para. 3, 2013, Critchley). After the accomplishment revealed from the graphic novel Monorama, it’s first incursion, the firm’s catalog of graphic novels has increased, meaning that it is a popular market (para. 4-5, 2013, Critchley). As a result, Larragoiti explains that “Resistencia opened the way for the graphic novel in Mexico…it has been a success and it has given Resistencia a push and a window into the market…” (para. 16, 2013, Critchley) where the image below is an example of their work.

Then, the creation of television has offered many individuals, such as Mexicans to become informed with information through this media which is “…a system for reproducing an actual or recorded scene at a distance on a screen by radio transmission, usually with appropriate sounds; the vision of distant objects obtained thus.” (para. 2, Ensign). Prior to its existence in Mexico, it began with an experimental television transmission conducted by Francisco Javier Stavoli in 1931 from Cuemavaca to Mexico City (para. 1, Barrera). Afterwards, Guillermo Gonzalez Camarena also experimented within this domain by first building his own monochromatic camera and that this study has lead him to develop a Trichromatic system which evoked the creation of colour television and that his achievement was confirmed in 1940 with the first patent in this domain (para. 1, Barrera). Next, Camarena created XE1GGC-Channel 5 in 1946 which was the first Mexican experimental television station to begin with weekly transmissions (para. 1, Barrera). As a result, with the breakthrough of television and its regulations applied, “…television in Mexico has been dominated by a handful of powerful individuals and family groups.” (para. 5, Barrera) which gradually made its way available to the general public. Eventually, after many conflicts between privately own sectors, the media company Televisa became a “…dominance in Mexican television…[and] provides over 12,000 hours of television programming each year…” (para. 14, Barrera). For instance, television plays a major role in influencing one’s lifestyle in Mexico such that it is a cause for obesity where “Mexico is restricting television advertising for high-calories food and soft drinks, as part of its campaign against obesity, the government says.” (para. 1, 2014, BBC) and the main television programming of soap operas (para. 14, Barrera). Through the observation of causality in television, it has been noted that it offers great strength in broadcasting information. In terms of the film industry in Mexico, its growth has gone through a great journey to become its success in the modern day. Prior to diving into the culture of Mexican film, the creation of a film comprises various elements from its predecessors which include books that are similar to a screenplay, music which are the original soundtrack scores found in movies and photography such that a film is composed of moving images (Media Theory, January 14, 2015). First, during the United States invasion in 1847 to 1848, Mexico and United States developed a not amiable companionship after Mexico lost half of its territory by the end of the war (p. 3, 2014, Serna). However, the turn around was that there were many American films welcomed and started to screen in Mexico, which brought a shift in the Mexican culture known and the Yanqui invasion (p. 3, 2014, Serna). Noted by Kristin Thompson, this metaphor “…would become almost universal in discussions of expanding the American film trade abroad.” (p. 3, 2014, Serna). While the films were brought from American corporations, they were injected with high level fidelity in technological advancements which has impressed Mexicans when they watched the films in movie theatres, schools and other venues (p. 1, 2014, Serna) and lead Mexico in learning the techniques to grow in the film industry. For instance, Latin American films dominated prior to the Golden Age where the production rate in 1940 and 1955 releases 100 features a year which brought its recognition to the sixth largest industry in film, while Hollywood in United States could not make its comeback until after World War Two (para. 2, The Americas: !Accion!; Mexico's film industry). As a result, with the growing population in film, the Mexican Institution of Cinematography even brings “…portable screens to neighbourhoods that lack cinemas, holding free screenings for Mexican flicks.” to bring awareness of films and encourage people to see more at theatres rather than pirating them online (para. 7, The Americas: !Accion!; Mexico's film industry). Thus, as seen in the poster and promotion video below, the Mexico International Film Festival and Awards has been created to celebrate the importance of the film industry in Mexico and to show the innovation of arts and cultural community of Mexico (para. 2, Mexico Film Festival).

In order to provide similar meaningful experiences, translation is often required to enhance culture, such as in the film industry that gets exported to various countries. Here, translation will be used as first “…the process of turning one language into another”, the second being “…the expression or rendering of something in another medium or form…” while the third is the “…transformation, alteration, change; changing or adapting to another use; renovation.” (para. 1, 2007, Lund). For instance, the Latin American movie titles of Knight of the Night, Super Agent 86 and The End of Time, “…are the literal translations of the Spanish titles given to The Dark Knight, Get Smart and The Happening…” where the creative translations used by Hollywood are to lure the large number of important foreign audiences (para. 2, 2008, Hawley). Furthermore, with the cultural differences and language barriers, there are times that are necessary to alter the titles in order to not offend or scare the audience (para. 7, 2008, Hawley). For instance, a moviegoer indicated that the title The Dark Knight, known as Caballero Oscuro in Spanish is considered too scary which might confuse the movie watchers in seeing a horror movie (para. 10, 2008, Hawley). Alternatively, even though there is the aspect of titles getting lost in translation, it is necessary to have meaning in another language otherwise there is no interest to the movie and that the plot cannot be given away within the title (para. 20-21, 2008, Hawley). An example can be seen in “Theima & Louise, a buddy film that shocked moviegoers with its suicide ending, was named An Unexpected End in Mexico.” (para. 22, 2008, Hawley). In the genre of superheroes, they have become an iconic language to Mexicans, where this concept consists of a broad meaning of kind, sort and style for a particular domain (para. 1, 2003, Clinton). The main spark began in the 1990s upon an explosion of companies that began to create native Mexican superhero characters (para. 1, 2002, Mexican Superheroes and Villains). In terms of design, in the genre of superheroes, they are often seen with masks, known as masked wrestler characters, which is their equivalent of the WWF wrestlers (para. 4, 2002, Mexican Superheroes and Villains) in alluding to one of Mexican’s famous sport in pro wrestling. As for the characters’ personalities, they would fight monsters and criminals instead of with wrestlers in a ring where examples composed of the Blue Demon, Tinieblas and Mil Mascars to name a few (para. 4, 2002, Mexican Superheroes and Villains) as portrayed below.

Hence, this big superhero on the outside, but kind hearted on the inside can provide delight to its people and potentially spread universally across the globe.
